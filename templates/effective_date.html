Effective Dates

Your effective date determines how far back VA pays you — it's the difference between months and years of back pay. Understanding how effective dates work is one of the most financially significant parts of VA claims.

Why effective dates matter: a real example
A veteran files a claim on January 1, 2020 for a knee condition. VA takes two years to decide and grants 30% effective January 1, 2020 — the filing date. At 30% (single, no dependents), that's roughly $524/month. Two years of back pay = ~$12,576 in a lump sum on top of ongoing benefits.

If the veteran had waited to file — or if VA incorrectly set the effective date to the decision date — that $12,576 disappears. (2024 rates, illustrative only)
The General Rule

Your effective date is the day VA receives your claim — or the day you become entitled to the benefit, whichever is later. For most veterans, this is simply the date your application was stamped received at a VA regional office or submitted on VA.gov.

This is why filing immediately matters. Even if your claim takes 18 months to decide, your back pay runs from the filing date, not the decision date.

How to File an Intent to File (ITF)

An ITF locks in your effective date for up to 12 months while you gather evidence. It costs nothing and takes under 5 minutes. You must complete and submit your formal claim within the 12-month window or the ITF expires.

Clear and Unmistakable Error (CUE)

CUE is the mechanism for correcting final VA decisions that contained a legal or factual error so obvious it would have changed the outcome. A successful CUE claim assigns the effective date of the original erroneous decision — meaning back pay can go back 10, 20, or 30+ years.

Common Mistakes That Cost Veterans Back Pay
  • Waiting to file until you have all your evidence — file the claim or ITF first, then gather evidence. Every day you wait costs back pay.
  • Not appealing within one year of a denial — after one year, the decision becomes final and an appeal no longer preserves the original effective date.
  • Withdrawing a claim instead of letting it be decided — a denial can be appealed; a withdrawal abandons the effective date permanently.
  • Missing the ITF expiration — if your ITF lapses before you file the formal claim, you lose that protected date and must start from the new filing date.
  • Not filing for secondary conditions at the same time — secondary conditions filed later get the new filing date, not the date of the primary condition.
  • Accepting the rating without checking the effective date letter — VA sometimes sets effective dates incorrectly. Always verify the date in your rating decision letter against your actual filing date.
