What is a nexus letter? It's a medical opinion from a treating physician or specialist stating that your condition is at least as likely as not (50%+ probability) connected to your military service. Without it, VA often denies claims as "not service-connected" even when the connection is obvious. Most doctors have never written one — this letter tells them exactly what to write.

Required VA language — the opinion must include one of the following phrases:
  • "It is at least as likely as not…" (50%+ — meets VA threshold)
  • "It is more likely than not…" (51%+ — stronger)
  • "It is highly likely…" (even stronger)
The opinion must also include a rationale — a brief explanation of why the doctor reached that conclusion. An opinion without rationale will be rejected.

Print this letter and bring it to your next appointment. Ask the doctor to write their opinion on their letterhead and sign it. Then submit it as evidence with your VA claim or appeal.

Tips for getting a strong nexus letter
  • Your treating physician is best. A doctor who has treated you long-term is more credible than a one-time evaluator.
  • Specialists carry more weight. An orthopedic surgeon's opinion on a back injury outweighs a GP's.
  • The magic phrase is "at least as likely as not." The VA only needs 50% probability — not certainty. Make sure your doctor uses that exact language.
  • Rationale is required. The opinion must explain why the doctor believes the connection exists. A bare conclusion is not enough.
  • Private DBQs are powerful. Ask your doctor to complete a Disability Benefits Questionnaire (DBQ) for your condition — VA forms that structure the medical opinion exactly how raters read it.
  • If your doctor won't write one, independent medical opinion (IMO) services exist — VSOs can refer you, or search for "IMO for VA claims."
